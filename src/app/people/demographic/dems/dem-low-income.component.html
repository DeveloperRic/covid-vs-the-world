Article Author: Brooke Heath

March 30, 2020


Everyone has faced at least some form of financial stress in their lives. Whether it was a late phone bill, being laid off from work, or being evicted from your home. Believe it or not, we all have bills to pay.

Some families or individuals live from paycheque to paycheque. They perceive time in either biweekly or weekly segments. “I can’t afford groceries this week, I don’t get paid for another week!”; a situation that many families experience on a regular basis. Let me tell you a little bit about my experience living on my own at 20 years old with no parental support. At the start of the what we now know as a global pandemic, things were already a little rough. Being an indigenous student, I get a monthly allowance (approx. one thousand dollars). That money is supposed to pay my rent, my meals, and any personal bills that I would have. Although since my rent increased from four hundred to seven hundred, I was short every month. I started a credit account and quickly began to accumulate debt, which I would not be able to pay off in time due to a mental health disability. I can only imagine that hundreds or even thousands of others are in the same boat as I am. COVID-19 has disrupted the normal functioning of our society to an extreme.

Once the COVID-19 virus spread internationally, schools began to move to an online format and cancelled face-to-face instruction and workplaces sent their employees to work from home. Students who learn by engaging with the professors would be at a disadvantage. Non-essential businesses (restaurants, malls, bars) shut down, taking away income to many student and non-student workers. Many students who are living on their own have to work in order to make ends meet. Closing down workplaces puts thousands at a disadvantage. As summer approaches, those in school look for jobs to support themselves while not studying. With local businesses being shut down, students can’t do this. How will students from low-income families afford rent, groceries, bills and other essentials?

The government offered to help the millions of Canadians who are facing unemployment by giving employers 75% of the regular wage so that they can pay their employees. Will it suffice? Only time will tell.
